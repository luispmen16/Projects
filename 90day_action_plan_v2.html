Daily Protocol
Non-negotiable minimum. 20 minutes total.
Morning · 5 min
Physiological Sigh
Double inhale through nose — short inhale, then a second top-up — followed by a long slow exhale through the mouth.
Repeat 5 times. Every morning, before screens.
Proven fastest method to downregulate stress response. Stanford Huberman Lab.

Mindfulness
The neurological foundation. Builds the gap between trigger and response.
Why it works for your pattern
Interoceptive Awareness
Your freeze starts in the body before it reaches the mind. Mindfulness trains you to detect the early signal — chest tightening, breathing shift, shoulder tension — before the prefrontal cortex goes offline.
8 weeks of practice produces measurable prefrontal cortex thickening and amygdala volume reduction. Harvard, Sara Lazar.
Practice 1 · Daily · 10 min
Breath Awareness
    1
    Sit comfortably
    Eyes closed or soft gaze downward. Spine upright but not rigid.
    2
    Focus on breath sensation
    Air at nostrils. Chest rising. Pause between exhale and next inhale. Physical, not conceptual.
    3
    When mind wanders — return
    Without frustration. Each return is one rep of the skill. This IS the practice.
Practice 2 · 3x week · 15 min
Body Scan
Lie down. Move attention slowly from feet to head. Notice sensations — tension, warmth, numbness, constriction — without trying to change them.
Why this matters most for you: Builds the interoceptive vocabulary to catch freeze signals in real time. Do in calm conditions first.
Practice 3 · In-the-moment
Mindfulness of Emotions
When fear or avoidance arises — instead of suppressing or analysing it, turn toward it with curiosity:
Where in my body?
What shape?
Temperature?
What would it say?
Emotions observed with curiosity lose intensity faster than emotions suppressed. Affect labelling reduces amygdala activation directly.

Your Distortions Map
The specific errors identified in your pattern
Double Standard Thinking
Rules applied to yourself that you would never apply to others. The primary distortion in your profile.
"Would I think this about a respected peer? No. Why is the standard different for me?"
Fortune Telling
"If I push, he'll react badly." Predicting negative outcomes without evidence — and using the prediction to justify avoidance.
Should Statements
"I should not need help." "I should have predicted his response." Rigid rules that create guilt and restrict action.
Catastrophising
"If I lose this relationship, I lose the opportunity." Overestimating the consequences of asserting a need.
Core intervention

Every time "I shouldn't need help" fires:
"If a talented CFO I respected came to me with this same situation — would I think less of them?"
You already know the answer. Use it.

Loss Intolerance &
Investment Paralysis
The same schema. A third domain.
What you said
"I will fail and lose everything. I feel sick on losing."
Two distinct things compressed into one statement. They have different origins and require different interventions.
Two Layers
Layer 1 — Cognitive
"Lose Everything"
All-or-nothing thinking. The mind collapses a continuous range of outcomes — partial loss, breakeven, gain — into a single catastrophic endpoint. It reacts to the worst-case tail as if it were the probable case.
This is the same catastrophising and fortune-telling distortion identified in the assertiveness domain. The mechanism is identical. The domain has changed.
The actual expected value of the investment is irrelevant. The mind has already arrived at catastrophe before the analysis completes.
Layer 2 — Somatic
"I Feel Sick"
Not a cognitive event. A physical threat response. The body reacting to financial loss as if loss were genuinely dangerous.
In the early environment, loss of control led to physical punishment. Financial loss is, neurologically, a loss of control over resources and future. The nervous system does not distinguish. It fires the same response.
The nausea is not irrational. It is historical data misfiring in the wrong context.
The Loop
Self-Defeating Cycle
1

Uncertainty = danger. Financial commitment = loss of control.
2

Any loss = "lose everything." Threat response activated.

The PE Strategy
Connection
This is not peripheral
Private equity specifically requires what the schema most resists:
    Committing large capital to illiquid positions with uncertain multi-year outcomes
    Holding through drawdowns without premature exit at the worst moment
    Irreversible decisions — acquisitions, capital structure — under uncertainty
    Tolerating the J-curve — position looks worse before it looks better
The personal development work on loss tolerance is a prerequisite for executing the PE strategy — not background preparation. Run these two tracks in parallel.
The Reframe
"This physical response is real. It is also historical data, not current assessment. My body is reacting to a threat encoding from a very different context. The loss of a position is not the same as the loss of safety."
Graduated Loss
Exposure Practice
The specific intervention for this domain. Same logic as the assertiveness hierarchy.
The Protocol
1
Take a small position
Financially irrelevant if lost entirely. The amount must feel real but not threatening to your security.
2
When loss occurs — allow the response
Do not suppress the nausea. Do not immediately analyse. Let the somatic response come.
3
Apply mindfulness tools
SBNRR. Breath. Observe the nausea with curiosity, not alarm. Where is it? What shape? Does it move?
4
Notice it passes
You are intact. The response peaked and subsided. Record this.
5
Record prediction vs reality
How bad did I predict it would feel? How long did I predict it would last? What actually happened?
6
Increase gradually
Only as somatic response reduces. Never jump ahead. The nervous system updates slowly and through repetition.
Purpose: not return generation. Nervous system retraining. The body learning that loss is survivable.

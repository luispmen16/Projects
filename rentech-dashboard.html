Chapter I
Timeline

From Cold War codebreaker to the greatest investor in history — a 50-year journey built on one radical insight: signal processing is signal processing, regardless of domain.

1938

Jim Simons Born, Newton MA

Mathematical ability from age 4. MIT undergrad in 3 years, Berkeley PhD in theoretical math. Elected high school class president — the extroverted mathematician.

1964

The Paper That Became Renaissance

At the NSA's civilian codebreaking unit (IDA), Simons publishes the probabilistic market trading blueprint — 24 years before the fund exists.

1967

Fired for Vietnam Op-Ed

Writes New York Times piece opposing the war. Doubles down with Newsweek. Fired at 30 with wife and three children.

1968

Chairs Stony Brook Math Dept

Builds one of the world's top math departments with Rockefeller funding. Assembles the talent base that later builds Medallion. Develops the Chern–Simons theory.

1978

Leaves Academia · Founds Monometrics

Opens in a Long Island strip mall next to a pizza joint. The math world is shocked: "He sold his soul to the devil."

1982

Renaissance Technologies Founded

With Howard Morgan: 50% quant trading + 50% venture capital. Howard later spins out the VC work — it becomes First Round Capital, a 50x fund.

1988

Medallion Fund Launched

Named after the collective mathematical prizes of the team. The greatest investment vehicle in history begins with ~$27M AUM.

1990

77.8% Gross · Year One

Berlekamp buys out Ax and cracks the key insight: trade far more frequently. Apply Kelly Criterion bet sizing. The machine begins to work.

1993

Brown & Mercer Join from IBM

Peter Brown (PhD adviser: Geoffrey Hinton) and Bob Mercer arrive from IBM speech recognition. They build the unified all-asset model and the equities engine.

2000

128% Gross — Tech Bubble Bursts

Markets collapse. Medallion soars. Proof of true uncorrelation: the model earns most precisely when human investors are most afraid.

2003

Medallion Closes to All Outsiders

All external investors expelled. Carry raised to 44%. The fund becomes exclusively employees and select alumni. AUM capped intentionally.

2008

152% Gross — Financial Crisis

The financial world melts down. Medallion's two best years are 2007–08. The thesis is validated across every conceivable market environment.

2009

Simons Retires · Brown & Mercer Lead

AUM raised from $5B to $10B with no performance drop. The Peter/Bob era (2010–2022) averages 77.3% gross — better than Simons' own era.

Chapter II
Strategy
"We're right 50.7% of the time — and we make billions that way."
— Bob Mercer, Co-CEO · Renaissance Technologies
1

Signal Processing, Not Investing

The model never reads a balance sheet or forms a thesis. It treats markets as data streams — identical to NSA codebreaking and IBM speech recognition. The math is the same. Only the input differs. No one at Medallion could tell you what stocks they own at any moment.

2

One Model for Everything

Brown & Mercer's pivotal contribution: a single unified model for currencies, commodities, and equities. Every researcher sees all the code. Every improvement benefits everyone equally. No other major quant fund operates this way — all peers use siloed teams.

3

Hidden Markov Models

Identify the "state" a market is in and predict future states from observed patterns — without needing to explain why. The same foundation underpins modern large language models. Peter Brown's PhD adviser was Geoffrey Hinton (the godfather of deep learning).

4

Trade What No Human Would

The model finds correlations no analyst could dream up — including relationships that make zero intuitive sense but are statistically persistent. When you trade the unintuitive, there are no competitors on the same side. This is the true alpha source.

5

Kelly Criterion & Bet Sizing

A 50.7% edge is worthless in 1 trade. Across 150,000 daily trades with optimal Kelly sizing: billions. Never bet so much that a losing streak destroys the fund. Berlekamp's insight: trade frequency × small edge = compounding dominance.

6

Volatility Is the Harvest

Best years: 2000 (+128%), 2007 (+136%), 2008 (+152%), 2020 (+149%). When humans panic-sell, the unemotional model buys at the right price. Renaissance is the house in a casino — market crises maximise the edge against panicking humans.

Sharpe Ratio Comparison · Base-100 Scale

What is Sharpe Ratio? Return earned per unit of risk taken. Formula: (Fund Return − Risk-Free Rate) ÷ Volatility. A ratio of 1.0 means you're earning 1 unit of return per unit of risk. Above 2.0 is considered exceptional for any fund. Above 3.0 is almost unheard of over a long track record.

The Fee Architecture as Incentive Machine

Since 2003, there are no outside investors. The GP and LP are the same people. So why charge 44% carry at all?

It functions as a value transfer mechanism: ~49% of annual returns flow to current contributors (via fees + carry), while ~51% accrue to the tenured LP base. Newer employees are heavily GP-weighted. Retirees are purely LP. As you age in the firm, your balance shifts from contributor to owner.

Structurally analogous to academic tenure — and it elegantly solves the defection problem. Why leave? The maths says stay.

Chapter VI
How to Build One

First Principles Thinking Applied

Strip every assumption away. Ask: what is fundamentally, probably true here? Ignore what everyone else does. Reconstruct from the ground up. This is how Simons actually built it — he was not copying a fund. He was solving a completely different problem and discovered it also made money.

Phase 1 · Strip the Assumptions
Assumption to Strip

"You need finance expertise to invest."

Fundamentally true: you need to understand risk, not companies. The market is a stream of data emitting signals. The question is who is better at signal processing — a financier or a physicist? Renaissance answered that 40 years ago. Hire for signal processing. Finance intuition is a liability, not an asset.

Assumption to Strip

"You need a thesis to make a trade."

Fundamentally true: you need a statistical edge, not a story. Humans are narrative machines — stories make us trade confidently on weak evidence. A model with no opinion, a 50.2% edge, and 100K daily trades is more profitable than a brilliant analyst with 10 annual picks. Remove the human thesis layer entirely.

Assumption to Strip

"Bigger AUM = bigger profits = better."

Fundamentally true: every strategy has a capacity ceiling. Beyond it, you move markets, slippage destroys the edge, and returns fall. Medallion caps at $10–15B despite infinite institutional demand. Optimise for returns per dollar, not total dollars. Stay small intentionally.

Assumption to Strip

"You need institutional capital to start."

Fundamentally true: you need capital, but not from LPs. Simons started with $4M from a flooring company exit. The early fund was personal money and friends. Start with proprietary capital. Prove the edge first. Then decide if outside money is needed at all — it may hurt more than it helps.

Phase 2 · What Is Fundamentally True
Fundamental Truth

Markets are a noisy system — not a random one. There is signal.

Randomness has no pattern. Noise has signal buried in it. Every trade ever executed was made by a human — and humans follow persistent patterns of fear, greed, momentum, and anchoring. That pattern is permanent and exploitable. The question is only whether you have enough data and compute to extract it.

Fundamental Truth

A tiny persistent edge × volume × time = extraordinary wealth.

You do not need to be right 70% of the time. You need to be right 50.1% of the time, size bets correctly (Kelly), and repeat 150,000 times a day. The casino doesn't win every hand — it wins every year. Edge × Volume × Time is the only formula that matters.

Fundamental Truth

Talent stays where they own the output of their work.

Competition for the best people is won not by salary but by ownership structure. When a researcher's improvement to the model directly increases their own wealth — not a bonus decided by someone else — incentive alignment is perfect. This is the real secret of the LP/GP architecture.

Phase 3 · Concrete Build Steps
Step 1 · Edge Discovery First

Find your domain before your strategy.

What signal processing domain do you already understand deeply? Modern options include:

  • NLP sentiment from earnings calls, news flow, or social media
  • Satellite imagery (retail traffic, oil inventories, shipping)
  • Options flow as a leading indicator for equity moves
  • Cross-asset correlations in emerging or frontier markets (thinner, less competed)
  • Microstructure patterns in crypto (24/7, highly data-rich, fewer quants)

Test statistically before trading. Target: Sharpe > 1.5 in backtest with realistic transaction costs before risking live capital.

Step 2 · Data Infrastructure Before Models

Obsess over data quality above all else.

Strauss built the data layer before the model existed. This is the correct order. Clean, long, granular data is the actual moat — not the algorithm. Start with:

  • Tick-level price data (Polygon.io, Databento, Refinitiv)
  • Alternative data aligned to your chosen edge
  • A cleaning pipeline that treats every data point as potentially wrong
  • Version-controlled dataset snapshots — reproduce any backtest exactly
Step 3 · One Model, Not Many

Unify from day one. Avoid siloed strategies.

Most people build strategy A, then strategy B, and they never communicate. Brown and Mercer proved that everything is correlated — a unified model discovers cross-asset patterns that siloed models miss entirely. Use a single ML framework (XGBoost, LightGBM, or a neural net) treating all asset classes as features of the same prediction problem.

Step 4 · Kelly Sizing & Kill Switches

Risk management is not optional — it is the business.

Kelly formula: f* = (bp − q) / b. Use half-Kelly in practice to account for model error. Hard rules:

  • Never risk more than 1–2% of capital per independent trade idea
  • Hard automated kill switch: if daily drawdown exceeds X%, halt trading
  • Every backtest must include realistic slippage — assume you move the market
  • Stress test against 2008 and 2020 scenarios before going live
Step 5 · Structure & Talent

Design the incentive structure before you hire.

The LP/GP architecture is not an afterthought — it is the product. Principles:

  • Every team member must invest their own capital — skin in the game is mandatory
  • Ownership percentage increases with tenure and measurable contribution to the model
  • Hire people who have never worked in finance — no bad habits to unlearn
  • Keep under 20 people until you have a proven, scalable edge
  • Locate deliberately away from financial centres to reduce social contamination
Step 6 · The Realistic Roadmap

What a modern bootstrap looks like, phase by phase.

  • Year 0–1: Pick one edge. Collect 5+ years of clean data. Build backtest with realistic costs. Do not trade live until Sharpe > 1.5.
  • Year 1–2: Live paper trading with $50K–$500K personal capital. Validate live performance matches backtest within 20%.
  • Year 2–3: Open to 3–5 co-investors who understand the strategy deeply. No institutional capital until you have 3 years live track record.
  • Year 3+: Scale only strategies that maintain Sharpe as AUM grows. Cap size ruthlessly when returns begin to compress. Develop the next edge in parallel — always.
The honest constraint: Renaissance had 25 years of iteration before it worked reliably, Simons' NSA signal processing background, Berlekamp's Kelly mastery, Strauss's decade of data infrastructure, and Brown & Mercer's large-systems engineering from IBM — all converging at the right moment in computing history. The playbook is replicable in principle. In practice, the question is not "can I copy this?" but "what is my equivalent of each of those pieces?" Find your Berlekamp. Find your Strauss. Then find your edge. The sequence matters as much as the ingredients.

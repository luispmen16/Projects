The core thesis

Acquire energy-intensive and less energy-intensive industrial companies with multiple production units where the gap between actual and best-practice EBITDA is driven by poor energy procurement, absence of internal benchmarking, poor operational productivity, and underdeveloped commercial capability. Apply the BA Glass operational playbook — internal plant-vs-plant benchmarking, energy procurement, ZBB, management upgrade — to close that gap. Exit to a strategic buyer at a consolidation multiple 2–3 turns above the entry price. Target sectors in Iberia and Europe with €20–150m revenue family-owned businesses and a clear succession gap.

The seven original selection criteria
01
Knowledge edge
Your BA Glass + Inditex + GANT direct experience — sectors where your CV gives you a zero learning curve advantage over any financial buyer.
02
Energy lever
EBITDA impact available from professional energy management. Sectors where energy is 10–40% of cost and most family operators have done nothing systematic.
03
Export growth potential
Ability to grow revenue by expanding internationally. A company going from 20% to 50% export gets re-rated at exit regardless of EBITDA improvement.
04
China protection
Structural moat vs Chinese competition — geographic origin, transport cost barrier, regulatory certification, or resource monopoly. Sectors where China cannot easily compete.
05
Demand stability
Inverse of end-market cyclicality. Sectors serving food, regulation, or non-cyclical services score highest. Construction-dependent sectors score lowest.
06
Buyer pricing power
Inverse of buyer professionalism. Low-sophistication buyers (contractors, tile shops) allow margin sustainability. Professional procurement teams (BASF, Nestlé) erode it.
07
Fragmentation
Availability of acquisition targets and buy-and-build potential. Sectors with 50–200 family-owned SMEs give you a platform roll-up opportunity at entry multiples of 3–4x.
Four additional criteria added after deeper analysis
08
Supplier power
Inverse of raw material concentration and input cost volatility. Abundant commoditised inputs (clay, limestone) give you procurement leverage. Scarce single-source inputs (cork bark, specialty chemicals) give suppliers pricing power over you.
09
Substitution risk
Structural product substitution beyond China — cans replacing glass bottles, screw caps replacing cork, LVT replacing ceramic tiles in some segments. Secular, permanent, and independent of Chinese manufacturing. Must be assessed separately from China competition.
10
Capital intensity
Inverse of maintenance capex burden. Glass furnaces (€20–50m rebuild every 10 years) and lime kilns consume free cash flow and limit debt service capacity. Asset-light businesses retain more of their EBITDA as distributable cash.
11
Exit buyer depth
Number and quality of credible strategic acquirers at exit. Glass has four global buyers (Ardagh, Verallia, O-I, Vidrala). Some sectors have one. More buyers competing = higher exit multiple and lower execution risk on the exit itself.

Key insights from the combined analysis
Energy lever is your primary moat
The gap between a family SME on an unmanaged energy contract and a professionally run industrial with PPAs, gas hedging and load shifting is typically 15–30% of the energy bill. On a company where energy is 25% of cost, that is 4–8 percentage points of EBITDA. No other single lever produces this magnitude of improvement this quickly. Pamesa's 2024 supplier switch saved €85m in one year. This is your repeatable playbook.
The ranking changes when you weight your skills
On equal weights, natural stone and ceramics top the list. When you weight energy lever and capital efficiency — the things you are actually good at — ceramics Castellón moves to #1. This matters because sector attractiveness is not abstract. It is personal. A sector that is generically attractive but does not leverage your specific skills is less valuable to you than one that does.
Glass is your safest bet, not your highest return
Glass packaging scores highest on knowledge edge (5/5) and exit buyer depth (5/5). You can evaluate a glass target in 48 hours and you know exactly who will buy it from you. But secular alcohol decline and very high capex (furnace rebuilds at €20–50m) compress free cash flow. Ceramics and stone offer higher returns. Glass is your fall-back if the right ceramics or stone target does not emerge in year one.
The buy-and-build premium is where the real money is
A single ceramics producer at €30m revenue trades at 3–4x EBITDA. A group of four producers with combined €120m revenue, shared energy procurement, internal benchmarking and an established export programme trades at 6–8x EBITDA. The operational improvement gets you from 8% to 15% EBITDA margin. The consolidation premium multiplies what you get for that margin at exit. Both levers must work simultaneously.

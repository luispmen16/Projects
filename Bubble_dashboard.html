01 · Methodology

How the analysis was built

Five sequential steps from raw data to investment conclusions. Quantitative inputs drive the ranking; qualitative judgment is applied only at the final conviction stage.

1

Data collection

Ten-year price series downloaded via Python using sector exchange-traded funds as proxies. STOXX 600 sub-indices for Europe, SPDR S&P sector funds for the United States. All series indexed to 100 at January 2015.

2

Performance metrics

Computed compounded annual growth rate, total return, maximum drawdown, and current distance from ten-year peak price for each sector in both geographies.

3

HALO scoring

Applied Goldman Sachs capital intensity index scores — calculated from fixed assets relative to total assets, tangible assets per employee, and capital expenditure relative to sales — to each sector.

4

Composite scoring

Composite score equals HALO Score multiplied by the absolute distance from peak price. A higher score indicates high capital intensity combined with a more depressed valuation — the maximum opportunity combination.

5

Crisis overlay

Added a third lens: recession-defence characteristics including non-discretionary demand, inelastic pricing power, dividend yield, and low market beta. This lens operates independently of the HALO score.

02 · Analytical Framework

Three lenses, one conclusion

No single metric is sufficient. The conviction framework requires alignment across all three dimensions simultaneously to qualify as a high-conviction position.

The core insight: Europe's HALO sectors have not re-rated yet. United States HALO sectors partially have.

A bubble burst accelerates European HALO re-rating while simultaneously collapsing United States technology weights. Europe is both the hedge and the opportunity — the geography and sector combination most undervalued on this framework, before the rotation becomes consensus and valuation gaps close.

03 · Key Findings

What the data revealed

Four structural observations from ten years of sector price data across Europe and the United States.

01

The compounded annual growth rate gap is extreme

United States Technology delivered a 22.4% compounded annual growth rate over ten years versus 2.9% in Europe. This is not a performance difference — it is a bubble. Mean reversion from a spread this wide has historically been violent and rapid.

02

European defensives are most depressed

European Construction (−30%), Food & Beverage (−20%), and Utilities (−13%) show the largest gaps from their ten-year peak prices in the dataset. In every case the cause is sentiment and rate environment — not structural demand destruction. These gaps represent entry points, not warning signals.

03

United States HALO sectors already partially re-rated

Most high-HALO sectors in the United States sit within 5% of their ten-year peak price. The trade is partially closed on the United States side. The alpha is in European equivalents — same sector thesis, significantly cheaper, with a currency tailwind if the euro strengthens during a risk-off rotation.

04

Food & Beverage breaks the HALO model — and that is the point

With a HALO score of only 47, Food & Beverage would be excluded from a pure HALO screen. But its −20% distance from peak combined with maximum crisis defence score makes it a Tier 2 conviction holding on a bubble burst thesis. HALO is an artificial intelligence disruption lens, not a recession lens.

Summary

Ten Iberian names

Across five sectors

The private equity fund geography is not just thematically correct — it is the single most undervalued combination of sector and geography in this entire analysis.

The 2000 analogy: when the dot-com bubble burst, Nestlé, Unilever, and Danone were top-decile performers in the subsequent 36 months.

The structural logic is identical today. Capital that exits technology at inflated multiples needs a destination. European defensives — utilities, food and beverage, healthcare, telecom — offer predictable cash flows, hard assets, dividend income, and in most cases a significant valuation discount relative to their own ten-year price history. The rotation is not a prediction; it is a mechanical consequence of how institutional investors rebalance after a bubble deflates. The only question is the timing of the entry.

Methodology note

Exchange-traded fund data downloaded via the yfinance Python library using ten years of daily adjusted closing prices from January 2015 to May 2026. Compounded annual growth rate calculated as (end price / start price) to the power of (1 / number of years) minus one. Distance from peak calculated as (current price / maximum price over the period) minus one, expressed as a percentage. HALO scores sourced from Goldman Sachs European Equity Strategy research by Guillaume Jaisson (2025–2026), based on the weighted average of three sub-scores: fixed assets as a percentage of total assets, tangible assets per employee, and capital expenditure as a percentage of sales. Composite score for the opportunity ranking equals HALO Score multiplied by the absolute value of the distance from peak, divided by 1,000. This document is analytical in nature and does not constitute investment advice or a recommendation to buy or sell any security. All company and market data is indicative as of May 2026.

Analysis uses exchange-traded fund proxies: STOXX 600 sub-indices for Europe, SPDR S&P sector funds for the United States.

Cash Flow Analysis — Why Hasn't Debt Decreased?
⚠ The Core Problem: $540M of Debt, ~$99M of Cumulative FOCF Over 7 Years

WireCo has generated approximately $99M of cumulative free operating cash flow from 2021 through 2027F — an average of just $14M per year. Against $540M of debt and ~$45M of annual interest payments, this leaves virtually nothing for debt repayment. The term loan balance has remained essentially flat since the 2021 refinancing. Three structural forces have trapped WireCo in this leverage position: the rate cycle, working capital, and modest underlying FCF conversion.

The Three Reasons Debt Has Not Decreased
📈
1. The Rate Cycle Ambush

Interest expense jumped from $39M (2022) to $55.7M (2023) — a $16.7M increase — despite EBITDA being essentially flat at $136–139M. This absorbed the entire $2.7M of EBITDA growth in 2023 and more.

The $540M TL is floating rate (SOFR + spread). At peak Fed rates (~5.5% SOFR), the all-in rate reached ~9–10%, more than doubling the 2021 interest burden. This rate shock is now reversing — 2027F interest is projected at $36.7M vs. $55.7M in 2023, releasing ~$19M of annual cash.

📦
2. Working Capital Consumed Growth Cash

In the strong revenue years of 2021–23, working capital consumed an estimated $67M cumulatively. Revenue growth from $670M to $759M (+13%) required proportional build in receivables and inventory. WireCo's ABL borrowing base is linked to receivables — capacity contracts exactly when performance weakens.

Additionally, S&P explicitly flags that WireCo invested heavily in working capital in 2025 to support the recovery. This pushed FOCF negative even as EBITDA improved from 2024 trough.

🏗️
3. Thin FCF Conversion + No Dividends

Even at peak EBITDA of $139M (2023), FOCF was only $20M — a 14% conversion rate. Interest, capex, taxes, and working capital consumed 86% of EBITDA. This leaves virtually nothing for discretionary debt repayment beyond mandatory 1% amortization (~$5M/yr).

No evidence of dividends. S&P makes no mention of dividend payments throughout the 10-year history — likely prohibited by leverage covenants. Onex's return has been purely through eventual exit, not cash distributions. PE monitoring fees (~$5–8M/yr estimated) are the only cash flow to the sponsor.

Forward Cash Flow Outlook: A Narrow but Opening Window

Two tailwinds are converging in 2026–27 that materially improve FCF generation:

Interest decline: As SOFR comes down, annual cash interest should fall from $44M (2025) toward $37M (2027F), releasing ~$7M/yr of incremental cash.

WC normalization: After heavy 2025 investment, S&P expects working capital improvement in 2026, contributing to the step-up in FOCF from −$8M to +$7M (2026E) and +$25M (2027F).

However, even in the best-case 2027F scenario:

FOCF of $25M against $540M of debt = 4.5% FOCF/Debt ratio. This is inadequate to meaningfully reduce leverage before the November 2028 TL maturity.

Bottom line: WireCo will not pay down this debt organically. The 2028 maturity must be resolved through a sale or a full refinancing. The sale window is 2026–mid-2027.

Strategic Insight: WireCo + BBRG = Global #1 — But Antitrust Makes This Complex

A combination would create ~$1.2B+ revenue, complementary geographies (Americas-led WireCo + Europe-led BBRG), and a dominant position in specialty rope globally. Bekaert is under structural pressure to sell BBRG — it earns 5.8% ROCE vs. Bekaert's Steel Wire Solutions at 27%. The strategic logic is undeniable.

However: both companies compete directly in offshore mooring rope, crane rope, and mining hoist rope. Combined market share in specific certified specialty segments — particularly North Sea offshore rope — could reach 40–60%. The EU's DG Competition would require significant divestitures of specific product lines as a condition of approval. A direct JV is even more problematic and would likely be treated as a cartel structure by competition authorities.

The Right Approach
Not now — WireCo's 6.5× leverage makes a $500M+ acquisition impossible today
Post-exit — a new owner with a clean balance sheet pursues BBRG with antitrust remedies fully scoped and priced in from day one
No JV — any operational joint venture with a direct competitor creates cartel-structure risk
The value — BBRG optionality makes WireCo more attractive to any sophisticated buyer; the eventual deal is real, just not today
BBRG Divestiture — Real Optionality, but Patient Timing

Not signalled by Bekaert management. Across four earnings call transcripts (Q1 2025 – Q1 2026), there is zero mention of any plan to sell BBRG. The Q4 2025 CEO commentary explicitly frames M&A in the lifting & mooring segment as an acquisition opportunity — Bekaert is talking about buying, not selling, in this space. The Bexco acquisition (synthetic ropes) was just completed and described as well-integrated.

But the financial case for disposal is tightening. BBRG goodwill headroom fell from €345M (2024) to €243M (2025). ROCE sits at 5.8% against a cost of capital of approximately 9–10%. Every year of sub-WACC returns erodes the strategic rationale for keeping it inside Bekaert. If EBITDA misses forecast by 1% across the plan period, headroom drops another €77M — impairment becomes a live conversation by 2027.

WireCo experienced quality control failures in its steel rope business during 2024, losing market share in the energy segment. S&P confirmed these were substantially resolved by early 2025. This is now a resolved risk but illustrates that WireCo's operational execution has been imperfect — third-party buyers will price in a management execution discount.
MED
Raw Material & Energy Volatility — Asymmetric Exposure by Segment
WireCo's two main divisions carry fundamentally different input risks. The steel rope segment (~50% of revenue) is exposed to wire rod — a steel market commodity priced on iron ore and coking coal, not oil. Bekaert sources 80–90% of wire rod locally per region, mitigating logistics risk significantly; WireCo should be similarly positioned given its US vertical integration.

The synthetic rope and nets segment (~34%, including Euronete) is exposed to polymers — HDPE, polypropylene, UHMWPE (Dyneema), nylon — which are petrochemical derivatives directly linked to oil and gas prices. A sustained 20% oil price rise translates to a ~25–35% polymer feedstock cost increase within 4–6 weeks. This is the channel through which the Middle East/Iran conflict matters operationally — not through direct business exposure, but through upstream polymer pricing.

Energy (primarily electricity, ~7% of revenue for comparable players) is hedged short-term but a sustained electricity price shift is a structural margin risk for wire drawing operations.

Pass-through works, but has a dangerous time lag. Wire rod adjustments run through contractual mechanisms with 1–3 month lags. Polymer adjustments to fishing fleet customers on annual contracts can lag 6–12 months. During that window, synthetic margins compress directly. Bekaert's Q1 2026 earnings call confirms increasing customer pushback on pass-throughs, even as management describes the mechanism as "working well overall."
Opportunities
US Tariff Competitiveness — Structural Near-Term Tailwind
WireCo's vertically integrated US manufacturing is uniquely positioned under the current tariff regime. Steel rope buyers increasingly prefer domestic suppliers to avoid tariff-induced supply disruption and price uncertainty. WireCo has already increased domestic crane rope production capacity for this purpose. This is share gain at the expense of Asian importers — requiring no market growth, just pricing and availability advantage.
Infrastructure & Data Center Construction Cycle
US infrastructure legislation is driving a multi-year increase in crane activity — cranes are WireCo's strategic focus in high-performance rope. Data center construction, semiconductor fab build-out, and grid modernization are all crane-intensive, creating sustained multi-year rope replacement demand. The crane OEM market is already surging; the legislative and nearshoring wave provides a second leg through 2028+.
Mining Capex Supercycle (Copper, Lithium, Cobalt)
Energy transition requires massive increases in copper, lithium, and cobalt production. Mine openings and expansions directly drive demand for hoist ropes (WireCo's 9% mining segment, highest-margin specialty product). The copper deficit is structural, not cyclical — demand from EVs, grid infrastructure, and data centers creates decade-long tailwinds. Pure picks-and-shovels exposure.
Product Mix Shift — Path Back to 18–19% Margins
WireCo's deliberate exit from commodity wire and expansion in engineered, certified, high-performance products (crane rope, deepwater mooring, mining hoist) is the single largest value-creation lever. The 2021–23 period demonstrated 18–19% EBITDA margins are achievable at scale. S&P currently forecasts only 13–14% — closing even half of this gap would add $20–25M of EBITDA, moving EV by $120–200M at 7–8x, and improving equity value by that full amount.
Interest Rate Tailwind — $19M of Annual Cash Flow Releasing
Cash interest is expected to fall from $55.7M (2023 peak) to $36.7M by 2027F as SOFR normalizes — releasing $19M of annual cash on a fixed EBITDA base. This alone could move FOCF/Debt from 1.3% (2026E) toward 6–8% by 2027–28, materially strengthening the company's liquidity and leverage optics for any sale process.
BBRG Acquisition Optionality (Post-Exit)
Bekaert is under structural pressure to divest BBRG (5.8% ROCE vs. 27% for Steel Wire Solutions). A new WireCo owner with a clean balance sheet could pursue BBRG to create the global #1 in specialty ropes. This bolt-on potential significantly enhances WireCo's attractiveness to any buyer — a strategic or PE acquirer paying for the platform, not just the current earnings.

Bottom Line Assessment

Business quality: Genuinely sound industrial asset with durable niche positions, ~80% consumables, and a credible strategic shift toward high-performance rope. Not a structurally declining business.

The problem is the capital structure, not the business. $540M of debt against $88.5M of EBITDA leaves no equity value at market multiples. Onex has been on the wrong side of the leverage trade throughout most of the hold.

Key insight on why debt didn't decrease: Floating-rate debt during a historic rate cycle was devastating. The $16.7M jump in interest expense from 2022 to 2023 alone offset the entire EBITDA growth in those peak years. WireCo was generating good EBITDA at the operating level but the interest bill consumed it.

Best outcome: Strategic buyer paying 9–10× on synergies (Kiswire the most logical). The BBRG angle makes WireCo even more compelling as an acquisition platform.

Watch: H1 2026 EBITDA. If trending toward $45–50M (annualizing $90M+), the sale process opens with credibility. If disappointing, leverage risks re-entering the danger zone and the exit window may close permanently.

The Core Diagnosis: A Good Business Trapped by a Bad Capital Structure

WireCo's end markets — oil & gas, mining for battery metals, crane-intensive data center construction, fishing — are structurally growing. The problem is not the business. It is $540M of floating-rate debt at 6.5× EBITDA that consumes every dollar of cash flow and prevents any investment in growth. The goal is surgical: remove the leverage constraint, then redirect all energy toward the growth agenda. These are two separate problems requiring two separate solutions.

⚠ Antitrust Constraint on Any Strategic Deal with a Direct Competitor

WireCo + BBRG (full acquisition or JV) — HIGH risk. Both compete directly in offshore mooring rope, crane rope, and mining hoist rope. In the North Sea offshore segment alone, combined market share could reach 40–60%. The EU's DG Competition would require significant divestitures of specific product lines before approving. A JV is even more problematic — competition authorities treat JVs between direct competitors as potential cartel structures, sometimes more harshly than outright acquisitions. This deal is not impossible but requires priced-in divestiture conditions from day one.

WireCo + Kiswire (full acquisition) — MODERATE risk. Kiswire is overwhelmingly Asian (50%+ revenue in Korea, limited Western certified-specialty presence). Geographic non-overlap in the US is substantial — Kiswire has almost no US manufacturing. Main scrutiny would be in specific European specialty segments. Likely approvable with modest divestitures or behavioural commitments.

Kiswire minority stake (30–40%, no operational control) — LOW risk. A financial investment without board representation or access to commercially sensitive information typically does not trigger a full merger review in most jurisdictions. This is why the minority stake route is not just financially attractive — it is the antitrust path of least resistance. This is the right structure for a near-term strategic partnership.

Bottom line: do not structure any deal with a direct competitor as a JV or operational partnership. Use a minority financial stake in the short term. Full strategic combinations are for post-exit, with antitrust remedies fully scoped and priced in from the start of negotiations.

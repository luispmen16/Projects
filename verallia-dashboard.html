Verallia FY2025 — Executive Summary  ·  Based on the URD 2025 annual report and FY2025 results presentation (25 February 2026). Figures in €M unless stated.
1 · The situation — a year of structural compression
P&L
Revenue fell while costs rose — the worst combination
Revenue declined 3.6% to €3,332M despite organic volume growth in all four quarters. The problem is pure price: average selling prices fell faster than volumes grew, while the cost of sales simultaneously rose +0.5% to €2,753M. Every cost line moved in the wrong direction. Gross margin collapsed 330 bps to 17.4%. SG&A rose +14.3%. Adj. EBITDA fell 17.8% to €692M (20.8% margin, −360 bps). Net profit fell 62% to €91M — EPS €0.77 vs €2.01 in 2024.
Cash
Free cash flow doubled — but for the wrong reasons
FCF rose from €83M to €166M, but the quality is poor. Operating cash flow actually fell 9% to €534M. The improvement came entirely from cutting CapEx by €64M (a deferral, not a structural gain) and lower tax payments. Working capital deteriorated — the cash conversion cycle widened 6 days as inventory built and payables compressed. Dividends of €200M (221% of net income) consumed all FCF and more, pushing net debt up €63M to €1,861M and leverage from 2.1× to 2.7×. The business generated cash but could not retain any of it.
Cycle
Six years of disruption have permanently shifted the cost base
Measured against 2019 (the last year of structural normality), the 2019–2025 cycle tells a damaging story. Selling prices rose +30.9% but industrial costs rose +32.5% — costs have permanently outpaced price recovery. Personnel costs per tonne packed rose +26.9% as wages increased +16% and productivity fell −8.6%. The profitability spread (ASP minus cost) peaked at €188/t (26.8%) in 2023 and has now compressed to €114/t (19.7%) — below the pre-crisis 2019 level of 20.6%. Verallia is structurally less profitable per tonne today than it was before the crisis cycle began.
2 · Root causes — three structural problems
① Price-Mix/Cost spread: −€236M EBITDA drag
Selling prices fell sharply in 2024–25 as customers pushed back after the 2022–23 inflation surge. Industrial costs fell more slowly, partly because Verallia's 2022 energy hedging contracts (locked at post-Ukraine crisis prices) remained valid through end-2025, keeping costs artificially elevated even as spot energy fell. The result: ASP down −17% from 2023 peak, costs down only −9% — a €122M gap that explains the bulk of EBITDA deterioration. This effect normalises in 2026 as the hedges expire.
② Labour productivity erosion: +27% cost/tonne since 2019
Headcount grew +11.3% (9,705 → 10,801) while production volumes stayed broadly flat vs 2019. Productivity collapsed to 510 t/person in 2023 and has only partially recovered to 548 t/person in 2025 — still 8.6% below 2019. Simultaneously, average wages rose +16% through collective agreements and mandatory increases across France, Germany, Brazil and UK. The combined effect: personnel cost per packed tonne rose from €86/t to €109/t — a structural embedded cost that does not compress easily.
③ Structural overcapacity in N&E Europe
N&E Europe's EBITDA margin fell to 14.5% in 2025 (CoS/revenue ratio: 89.9%), with Germany in structural demand decline driven by beer and sparkling wine consumption trends. Beer, sparkling wine, and spirits volumes all fell in 2025. Verallia has reduced German furnaces from 10 (2022) to a planned 6 (post-Essen closure), and the Cognac furnace in France will not be reconstructed. The 22 industry-wide furnace closures since end-2023 (~10% of European capacity) point to a structural reset — but the adjustment is still in progress.

Key tailwinds entering 2026
✓ Energy hedges resolved — the 2022 above-market contracts fully expired. Energy costs returning to market level, removing a headwind that depressed margins throughout 2024–25.

✓ Price carry-over fading — the negative price/mix effect was −€111M in H1 2025 and −€78M in H2, confirming the trend. Expected to approach zero by full-year 2026.

✓ Capacity adaptation in motion — Essen (Germany) and Cognac (France) restructuring will reduce fixed cost drag. ~50% of savings expected in 2026, 100% in 2027.

✓ LatAm momentum — Brazil growing, Campo Bom fully operational. LatAm already contributes 18% of group EBITDA at a 33% margin, and is expected to outperform Europe in 2026.
Key risks remaining
✗ Mix uncertainty — the shift from premium wine/spirits to food/NAB carries a structural ASP headwind that management cannot fully control. Mix was negative in both 2024 and 2025.

✗ CapEx underspend risk — cutting CapEx from 9.4% to 7.8% of revenue buys near-term cash, but defers furnace rebuilds in a business where glass furnaces last 12–15 years. The bill will come.

✗ Demand still fragile — management's base case for Europe is flat, not recovery. Geopolitical and trade uncertainties persist. Still wine and sparkling wine face structural consumption declines in key markets.

✗ Labour costs sticky — productivity at 548 t/person vs 599 in 2019. Wages will not fall. The +27% personnel cost/tonne vs 2019 is a permanent structural cost unless volumes recover to absorb the fixed base.
5 · Bottom line
→
2025 is likely the trough — but the path back to 24–25% margins is not automatic
Verallia is still a fundamentally strong business: #1 in Europe, 35 plants, 67 furnaces, 11,000+ customers, €692M EBITDA, ROIC above WACC, investment-grade rated. The 2025 results reflect a convergence of cyclical headwinds (price corrections, demand crunch, above-market energy hedges) that are now resolving. The structural tailwinds for 2026 are genuine. But the road back to 24–25% EBITDA margins — the historical norm before the crisis cycle — depends on three things happening simultaneously: selling prices stabilising (spread normalisation), productivity recovering as volumes grow, and the restructured European footprint absorbing demand more efficiently. If all three materialise, 2027 could see a meaningful margin recovery. If demand remains soft and mix continues shifting towards lower-value categories, the structural cost inflation of the 2019–2025 cycle may prove permanent.

1
Volume recovery masked by price — and higher costs made it worse
Verallia sold more glass in 2025 than in 2024 — volumes grew organically in all four quarters. Yet revenue fell 3.6% because average selling prices declined faster than volumes grew. More troubling, while prices fell, the cost of sales actually rose (+€14M, +0.5%) — driven by sticky raw material, energy and transport costs that did not deflate in line with selling prices. The result is a 330 bps collapse in gross margin from 20.7% to 17.4%. To make matters worse, SG&A increased +14.3% (+€24M) in a year of revenue compression, partly reflecting personnel cost inflation and takeover-related advisory fees — a cost line that should not grow when the top line is falling. This is not a volume problem. It is a price-cost spread problem compounded by cost base inflation across every line.
2
Cash generation is weak, not strong — the FCF doubling is misleading
Free cash flow doubled to €166M, which sounds positive, but the quality matters. Operating cash flow actually fell 9% to €534M — consistent with lower earnings. The FCF improvement came almost entirely from cutting CapEx by €64M and lower tax payments. Reducing CapEx in a capital-intensive industrial business (35 glass plants, 67 furnaces) is not free: it defers maintenance, furnace rebuilds, and technology upgrades that will need to happen. Meanwhile, working capital deteriorated — the cash conversion cycle widened by 6 days as inventory built and payables compressed. And on top of all this, dividends of €200M (221% of net income) consumed the entirety of FCF and more, causing net debt to rise €63M despite generating positive free cash flow.
→
The one genuine positive: entering 2026 with less headwind
The 2025 P&L is near a trough. The energy hedging penalty from 2022 contracts is fully resolved entering 2026. The Price-Mix/Cost spread, which drove −€236M of EBITDA damage, is expected to normalise towards zero as pricing carry-over fades and volume recovery continues. The structural capacity adaptation in Germany and France will reduce fixed cost absorption losses. And ROIC at 10.5% remains above WACC, meaning the business is still creating value — just very little of it. The path back to 24–25% EBITDA margins is visible, but it requires spread normalisation first.

1
2019–2021: stability — costs and prices move together, healthy spread
The business was broadly stable through the 2019 baseline years. Costs dipped slightly in 2020–21 on lower energy prices. The ASP-to-cost gap held at 20–24% of ASP. Volumes were at full capacity and production broadly matched sales.
2
2022–2023: the crisis — costs exploded, prices followed and overshot
Ukraine war detonated European energy markets. Industrial cost surged from €351/t (2019) to €513/t (2023) — +46%. Verallia passed costs through: ASP jumped from €443/t to €701/t (+58%), widening the spread to a peak of €188/t (26.8%) in 2023. That was the most profitable year precisely because prices overshot costs. But volumes began to fall as customers destocked.
3
2024–2025: prices fall faster than costs — and production exceeds sales
ASP fell from €701/t (2023) to €580/t (2025) — a −17% decline. Costs fell more slowly: €513/t → €465/t, only −9%. Spread compressed to €114/t (19.7%) — below 2019's 20.6% in % terms, meaning the business is structurally less profitable per tonne than before the crisis. Simultaneously, production outpaced sales by +109 kt in 2024 and +170 kt in 2025, building a 279 kt inventory surplus vs 2019 — costs already absorbed in P&L but cash not yet recovered.
!
The core profitability problem
Over the full 2019–2025 cycle, prices rose +30.9% but costs rose +32.5% — costs have permanently outpaced the price recovery. The 2022 energy hedges (locked at peak prices, valid through end-2025) kept costs artificially elevated even as spot energy fell. With those hedges now expired, the path to 24–25% EBITDA margins requires spread normalisation towards zero — which management has committed to as its 2026 target.

Why the EBITDA spread (−€236M) is wider than the revenue Price-Mix (−€189M): The gap of ~€47M reflects net cost inflation that was not recovered through pricing. Three drivers compounded this: (1) Personnel wage inflation across all geographies — France, Germany, Brazil and the UK all faced collective agreement increases, adding cost with no revenue offset; (2) Above-market energy costs throughout 2025, as Verallia's 2022 energy hedging contracts (locked at post-Ukraine crisis prices) remained valid until year-end — this structural penalty is now fully behind the company entering 2026, with energy costs returning to market level; (3) Higher CO₂ certificate costs from increased production volumes. Gross cost inflation before PAP savings was therefore significantly larger than the ~€47M gap implies — the PAP programme (+€45M) offset roughly half the underlying cost pressure.

Market outlook for 2026
Management view expressed at FY2025 results presentation
Europe — stability, not recovery
Continued soft consumption backdrop with no material rebound expected. Volumes broadly flat. Price carry-over effect from 2024–25 cuts will gradually phase out through the year — negative impact heaviest in H1, largely neutral by H2. Spread expected to normalise towards zero over the full year, though mix remains the key uncertainty (mix was negative in both 2024 and 2025 and is difficult to predict).

Latin America — outperform
More dynamic market, especially Brazil. Volume growth expected to continue. Beer and spirits strong. Campo Bom second furnace fully operational from H1 2026, supporting volume ramp.

Demand by end market
Non-alcoholic beverages and food: positive growth expected. Spirits, beer, still wine: stable to slightly down. Sparkling wine: continued headwinds. The progressive shift towards food and NAB within the mix is a structural trend the Group is actively targeting with its commercial and innovation strategy.

Geopolitical / macro
Trade uncertainties and FX volatility expected to persist. Guidance does not embed an upturn scenario — management explicitly positioned this as a cautious, self-help driven target.

Strategic rationale
Three levers: competitiveness · cash generation · asset efficiency
Why now — conjunctural vs structural
Management initially treated the post-2023 volume decline as cyclical and implemented temporary cold stops and capacity curtailments. After a strategic review in each European country, the conclusion shifted: in Germany and France Cognac, the demand reduction is structural, not cyclical. A definitive industrial response is now required rather than further temporary measures.

Germany — depth of the restructuring
In 2022 Verallia operated 10 furnaces in Germany. Post-plan, this falls to 6 — a 40% reduction in furnace count over three years. The 2024 and 2025 temporary adaptations already accounted for part of this adjustment. The Essen closure is the final structural step to redeploy the remaining business onto three competitive sites.

Capital Market Day
Management has indicated a Capital Market Day (moved from January 2026 to H2 2026) at which medium-term margin targets and the full strategic roadmap will be presented. The historical "normative" EBITDA margin range is 24–25%; the 2023 peak was above 28%. Management declined to commit to a specific target ahead of the CMD.

Key management messages — FY2025 results
Four priorities stated for 2026
① Competitiveness
Implement capacity adaptation plan (Germany, France, UK). Deliver enhanced PAP savings. Maintain capex at ~8% of sales.

② Cash generation
Target FCF of ~€220M (excl. restructuring). Energy cost headwind from 2022 hedges now fully behind the company — energy costs moving to market level in 2026. Cash tax expected to remain lower than historical levels.

③ Deleveraging
Leverage of 2.7× at end-2025 expected to decline. Dividend structure (max €20M cash-out) and FCF generation provide a clear path. Management has committed to maintaining investment-grade credit rating.

④ Industrial footprint adaptation
Structural decisions in Germany and France to be fully implemented in H1 2026. ~50% of savings to be realised in 2026, 100% in 2027. Q2 2026 results will include full detail on the plan and expected savings quantum.

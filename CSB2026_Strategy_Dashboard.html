Chapter 1

Strategy & Performance

The foundation: why our mental models determine organisational outcomes, how to define value and performance, and the two fundamentally different ways firms approach strategy — with profound implications for boards.

The Lens Problem: How Mental Models Shape Outcomes

The way managers perceive the world — their mental model or "cognitive lens" — determines how they interpret information, set priorities, and allocate resources. This is the insight behind the Upper Echelons Theory (Hambrick & Mason, 1984): organisational outcomes mirror the top team's cognitive lenses.

Boards must understand not only what management is doing, but how they are thinking. Two managers looking at the same data will reach different conclusions based on their worldview. Two boards reviewing the same strategy will ask fundamentally different questions.

System 1 Thinking
Fast, intuitive, automatic. Pattern recognition from experience. Efficient but prone to bias and blind spots. Where most strategic decisions actually get made.
System 2 Thinking
Slow, deliberate, reflective. Analytical and effortful. More accurate for novel situations. The board's role is to activate System 2 when management is operating on autopilot.
Board Implication

The board's first job is not to review strategy — it is to surface and interrogate the mental model behind the strategy. The lens shapes everything that follows.

Chapter 2

Strategy & Technology

How successive waves of digital technology have systematically restructured industries, disrupted competitive positions, and changed what strategic options are available — and why AI represents a qualitative break from previous waves.

The Pattern of Technological Disruption

Technology does not merely improve operations — it rewrites the rules of competition. Each major technological wave changes which activities create value, which capabilities matter, and which incumbents survive. Boards that treat technology as an IT matter rather than a strategic matter are systematically late.

The Core Pattern

Each tech wave first automates existing tasks (efficiency gain), then enables new tasks previously impossible (paradigm shift), then reshapes industry structure entirely (creative destruction).

Waves of Digital Technology

1950s–1970s
Mainframe Computing
Automation of back-office processes. Data processing at scale. Competitive advantage went to firms that could afford infrastructure. Centralised, scarce, expert-operated.
1980s–1990s
Personal Computing
Computing decentralised to the individual. Productivity transformation in knowledge work. Created new software industries. Microsoft, Oracle, SAP become strategic infrastructure.
1990s–2010s
The Internet & Mobile
Information asymmetries collapsed. Distribution costs → zero. Platform economics emerged. Winner-takes-most dynamics. Entire industry chains disrupted (media, retail, travel, finance).
2010s–Present
AI & Machine Learning
Machines learn from data rather than explicit programming. Cognitive tasks become automatable. The frontier shifts from information processing to judgement and prediction.

The Four Stages of AI

AI is not a single technology — it is a sequence of capabilities, each qualitatively more powerful than the last. Boards must understand which stage is relevant to their sector today, and what is coming next.

Stage 1
Symbolic AI
Rules-based. Human-encoded logic. Expert systems. "If-then" reasoning. Strong within defined rules; brittle when rules break down.
Stage 2
Machine Learning
Learns patterns from data. Statistical prediction. Requires labelled training data. Excels at classification, recommendation, fraud detection.
Stage 3
Generative AI
Creates new content (text, images, code, audio). Language models. Emergent capabilities. Transforms knowledge work productivity at scale.
Stage 4
Agentic AI
Takes autonomous action sequences. Executes multi-step tasks. Operates in real systems. The boundary between tool and agent begins to dissolve.

Why AI Is a Qualitative Break

Scale Without Cost
Marginal cost of AI intelligence approaches zero. Unlike human cognitive labour, AI scales without proportional cost. This breaks traditional productivity economics.
Cognitive Tasks Automated
Previous waves automated physical or routine tasks. AI automates judgement — the domain of professionals and managers. The impact reaches up the value chain.
Emergent Capabilities
AI systems exhibit capabilities their designers did not programme and cannot fully predict. This is not Risk — it is Absolute Uncertainty. Standard governance frameworks don't apply.
Speed of Transition
Previous waves played out over decades. AI capabilities are compounding quarterly. Boards calibrated to 3-year planning cycles are structurally too slow for this environment.
Strategic Question for the Board

The question is not "how do we use AI?" but "in a world where AI continuously changes what is possible, what kind of firm do we want to be?" This is a question of identity, not implementation.

Key Board Questions on Technology

1

Which of our current competitive advantages depend on human cognitive labour that AI will make commoditised or obsolete?

2

Which activities in our value chain does AI make newly feasible — that we could not do before?

3

At what stage of the AI development curve are our most dangerous competitors operating?

4

Does our management team have the cognitive model to navigate Absolute Uncertainty in AI, or are they applying Risk tools to a non-Risk problem?

Chapter 3

Geography & Global Strategy

Globalisation is not a single event — it is a sequence of strategic logics, each demanding different capabilities from firms and different oversight from boards. Understanding which phase your industry is in determines which strategic moves make sense.

Three Phases of Globalisation

The global economy has passed through three distinct phases, each with a different strategic logic. Critically, industries move through these phases at different speeds — a board member must know which phase their industry currently inhabits.

Phase 1 · 1950s–1980s
🌐
World as Market
"Sell globally what you make locally"
Firms export from home-base to international markets. Competitive advantage built at home, then deployed abroad. Multi-domestic strategies. Replication across geographies. Key capability: market entry and local adaptation.
Phase 2 · 1980s–2000s
🏭
World as Factory
"Make globally where it's cheapest"
Global value chains fragment. Production shifts to lowest-cost locations. Supply chains span continents. The firm orchestrates a global production system. Key capability: supply chain coordination and cost arbitrage.
Phase 3 · 2000s–Present
💡
World as Garage
"Innovate globally, everywhere"
Innovation and knowledge creation become globally distributed. Emerging market firms are not just consumers or manufacturers — they are innovators. Reverse innovation. Frugal innovation. Key capability: orchestrating global knowledge flows.

The Emerging Global World

We are entering a fourth condition — not yet a stable phase — characterised by the reversal of some globalisation assumptions:

Geopolitical Fragmentation
US-China decoupling, reshoring, friend-shoring. Global value chains reorganising around geopolitical blocs. "Efficiency" trading off against "resilience."
Digital Globalisation
Physical trade may slow, but data flows accelerate. Platform-based firms globalise instantly. Digital services cross borders without friction. A new form of globalisation with different winners.
Emerging Market Power
China, India, Southeast Asia no longer operate in Phase 1 or 2. They are Phase 3 innovators and global competitors. The strategic assumption that innovation flows West→East is obsolete.
Sustainability Constraints
Carbon costs, regulation, and consumer preference are rewriting the economics of long supply chains. ESG is not just ethics — it is changing the comparative advantages of geographies.

Geographic Strategy: Key Board Questions

The Central Tension

Globalisation historically offered efficiency through integration. The emerging world rewards resilience through diversification. These objectives are in direct tension. The board must decide which takes precedence — and understand the trade-off explicitly.

IKEA as the Strategic Laboratory

IKEA's global strategy illuminates all three phases simultaneously. Its core model (flat-pack, customer assembly, warehouse stores) was developed in Sweden — Phase 1 logic. Its manufacturing was progressively offshored and managed through a complex global supply chain — Phase 2. Its most recent expansion into India and digital required deep local innovation — Phase 3. The tension IKEA faces today — between its efficiency-built global supply chain and the need for resilience and local responsiveness — mirrors the dilemma every global board faces.

Chapter 4

Competitive Strategy & The Board

The board's role in competitive strategy is not to choose strategic positions — that is management's job. The board's job is to diagnose whether the strategy is coherent, whether it is creating value, and whether it will hold up under competitive pressure.

What Is Competitive Strategy?

Competitive strategy answers the question: why do customers choose us over our competitors? There are only three durable answers:

Position 1
Cost Leadership
Lowest delivered cost in the industry. Win by being most efficient. Sustainable only if cost advantage is structural, not operational.
Position 2
Differentiation
Offer something uniquely valuable that customers will pay more for. Sustainable only if willingness-to-pay advantage is durable.
Position 3
Focus
Serve a narrow segment with either cost or differentiation. Sustainable only if the segment has distinct needs the focuser uniquely meets.
Blue Ocean
Value Innovation
Simultaneously reduce costs and increase willingness-to-pay by eliminating industry assumptions. Create uncontested market space.

The vpc Lens on Competitive Strategy

The vpc framework makes competitive strategy precise. A competitive advantage must either:

Raise Willingness to Pay (v)
Customers value your product more than competitors'. Enables price premium or higher volume. Requires differentiation that is both real and perceived.
Lower Cost (c)
Produce at lower cost than competitors. Enables margin advantage at same price or price undercutting. Requires structural (not just operational) cost advantages.
Both: Value Innovation
The rare Blue Ocean move — raise v while lowering c by eliminating or reducing activities that don't drive customer value. IKEA's original model did exactly this.

The VRIO Framework: Is the Advantage Sustainable?

Not every competitive advantage is durable. The VRIO test determines whether a resource or capability constitutes a sustained competitive advantage:

TestQuestionIf No →
ValuableDoes it enable a higher v or lower c?Not a competitive advantage

Good Strategy vs. Bad Strategy

Richard Rumelt's diagnosis of strategic failure is essential board reading. Bad strategy is endemic — not because managers are lazy, but because the social dynamics of strategy processes systematically favour it.

The Board's Diagnostic Question

"What is the specific challenge this strategy is designed to address?" If management cannot answer this in two sentences, you do not have a strategy — you have a wish list. The board's job is to insist on the diagnosis before approving the plan.

Complementarity and Co-specialisation

Sustainable competitive advantage often comes not from individual resources but from complementarity — the way resources combine so that each increases the value of the others. This creates emergence: the system creates value that no component could create alone.

Co-specialisation is the extreme case: two resources become so mutually adapted that separating them destroys value in both. This is both a strength (hard to imitate) and a risk (strategic lock-in). Boards must understand which combinations create real emergence and which are merely aggregation — the difference is not always obvious from the outside.

Chapter 5

Corporate Strategy & The Board

Corporate strategy asks: what portfolio of businesses should we own, and why? The board's role is to rigorously test whether the corporate centre creates or destroys value — and to deploy non-predictive logic when the future cannot be predicted.

The Diversification Test

Porter's three tests for diversification remain the best filter for corporate strategy decisions. Any acquisition, expansion, or diversification move must pass all three:

1

Attractiveness Test: Is the industry structurally attractive enough to generate returns above cost of capital? Entering an unattractive industry destroys value regardless of execution.

2

Cost of Entry Test: Does the cost of entry (acquisition premium, greenfield investment) consume the future profits? If you pay for all the synergies upfront, there's nothing left for shareholders.

3

Better-Off Test: Will the combined entity be better than either business would be separately? This is the synergy test — and most claimed synergies are aggregation (additive), not emergence (multiplicative).

Synergy: Aggregation vs. Emergence

Most synergies are aggregation — 1+1=2 (cost cuts, shared services). Real value creation requires emergence — 1+1=3 (capabilities that combine to create something neither could alone). Boards must demand evidence of which type of synergy is being claimed before approving transactions.

Non-Predictive Logic: Navigating Absolute Uncertainty

When the future cannot be predicted — genuinely unknown unknowns — standard strategic planning fails. Non-predictive logic is the board's toolkit for absolute uncertainty. It does not try to predict outcomes; it builds resilience and optionality.

01
Precautionary Principle
When in doubt, don't — especially when the downside is catastrophic

Asymmetric logic: the cost of caution when caution was unnecessary is small. The cost of not being cautious when caution was necessary can be existential. Applied to irreversible decisions, novel exposures, and tail risks that cannot be quantified. Nassim Taleb's "black swan" framework is the modern version.

02
Stop-Loss Rule
Fix the maximum you are willing to lose before you begin

Pre-commit to exit conditions before emotional investment builds. The cognitive bias of sunk costs causes firms to persist in failing strategies long past rational stopping points. The stop-loss rule moves the exit decision to a moment of clear judgement, before the loss has been incurred. Boards should require stop-loss conditions on all major investments.

03
Via Negativa
Define what you will not do — and hold the line

In uncertainty, positive predictions fail. But negative predictions are more robust: knowing what to avoid is more reliable than knowing what will succeed. Strategic identity defined by what the firm refuses to do creates clarity and resistance to opportunistic drift. "We do not do X" is more durable than "we will be the leader in Y."

04
Principia
Act on principles rather than plans

When the future is unknowable, plans become obsolete before they are executed. Principles — clear, internalised decision-making guides — enable rapid, consistent responses to unexpected situations without needing to replan from scratch. They enable strategic coherence without strategic rigidity. What are our non-negotiables?

Ownership Structure as Invisible Diversification

A dimension of corporate strategy that boards often overlook: who owns the firm is itself a strategic variable. Ownership structure determines time horizons, risk tolerance, governance dynamics, and the range of strategic options available.

Public Shareholders
Quarterly earnings pressure. Diversified investors who don't need firm-specific risk-taking. Efficient capital markets but short-termism risk. Strategy must balance short- and long-term value creation.
Concentrated / Family Ownership
Long time horizons. Patient capital. Aligned interests (or not). Can make bold bets markets would not approve. Risk of entrenchment and principal-principal conflicts. IKEA: Ingka Foundation structure enables 50-year thinking.
Private Equity
Defined 3–5 year horizon. Value creation through operational improvement and financial engineering. Active governance. Alignment through co-investment. Risk of over-leverage and short-term extraction.
Sovereign / State
Geopolitical objectives alongside financial ones. Very long horizons. Can absorb losses public markets cannot. Strategic sectors receive patient capital. Risk of political rather than economic decision-making.
The Board's Ownership Lens

Every board strategy discussion happens within constraints set by ownership structure. A board that ignores these constraints is not being realistic about what strategic options are genuinely available. The best strategy for a publicly-listed firm is not the same as the best strategy for a family-owned firm — even in the same industry.

The Ultimate Choice

Everything in the course converges on a single strategic choice that every board and management team must ultimately make: Shape the future or Adapt to it. This is not merely a strategic preference — it is a commitment that determines which mental model, which tools, and which organisation design are appropriate.

The Fundamental Dichotomy

Two coherent and internally consistent approaches exist to strategy. They are not on a spectrum — they are genuinely different paradigms. Every element of the firm (organisation design, leadership style, planning process, governance approach) should be aligned with the chosen approach.

The IKEA Synthesis

IKEA is the recurring case study throughout the course because it embodies both approaches simultaneously — and the tension between them is the company's central strategic challenge today.

IKEA's Shaping Origins
Ingvar Kamprad did not analyse the furniture market and find a gap. He had a vision of "democratic design" — beautiful, functional furniture that ordinary people could afford. He shaped a new market. The flat-pack solution was discovered accidentally (a designer removed table legs to fit in a car). Serendipity enabled by commitment to a principle.
IKEA's Adaptive Excellence
Once the model was established, IKEA built extraordinary adaptive capabilities: supply chain optimisation, global sourcing, store format replication. The Sequential model (analyse market → design store → open → repeat) drove decades of global expansion.
IKEA's Current Dilemma
Digital disruption, sustainability pressure, urban customers who can't drive to warehouse stores — IKEA now faces the question again: Adapt (add e-commerce, small-format stores) or Shape (reinvent what home living means in 2030)? The answer determines the whole organisation.

Serendipity: The Strategic Role of the Unexpected

The course introduces serendipity not as luck but as a strategic variable. The most consequential strategic breakthroughs — IKEA's flat-pack, Post-it notes, penicillin — were not planned. They emerged when someone with a clear purpose encountered an unexpected event and had the mindset to recognise its value.

Serendipity ≠ Luck

Serendipity requires three conditions: a clear purpose (to recognise relevance), an open mind (to see the unexpected as opportunity rather than noise), and an organisation that allows exploration. You cannot plan serendipity, but you can create the conditions for it. Most firms systematically suppress these conditions through planning rigidity and performance pressure.

The Board's Most Revealing Question

"What has surprised you since our last meeting?"
The most diagnostic question a board can ask management

If management is never surprised, one of two things is true: either they are operating in an unusually predictable environment (rare), or they are not paying attention to the signals at the edge of their model (common). Surprise is the symptom of Absolute Uncertainty making contact with the firm. The board's ability to surface and process surprise is a governance capability in itself.

Synthesis & Vision

How to Approach Boards and Strategy

Six chapters converge on a single imperative: the board's ultimate function is not oversight of the past but governance of the future — and that requires a fundamentally different orientation than traditional governance frameworks assume.

The Board's Unique Strategic Function

Management operates in the domain of Risk — measurable uncertainty that can be planned for and managed. The board's distinctive, irreplaceable contribution is to operate in the domain of Absolute Uncertainty — the unknowable future that cannot be planned for, only navigated.

This reframes the entire governance relationship. The board does not exist to approve management's plans. The board exists to absorb the uncertainty that would otherwise paralyse management — and to give management the clarity of purpose and confidence of principle needed to act decisively in an uncertain world.

The EOS Model: Dynamics the Board Must Understand

The EOS model is deceptively simple — three letters. Its power lies in what happens between them. The most common governance failure is treating E, O, and S as three separate agenda items, when the strategic value lies entirely in their mutual interdependence.

Sequential Logic (E→S→O)
Environment is exogenous — a given. Management reads it, formulates Strategy to fit it, then builds Organisation to execute. The board validates the environmental reading and the strategic fit. Clean, linear, predictable. Works when the environment is stable.
Complex Logic (EOS Reciprocal)
All three elements co-evolve. Acting changes the environment. The organisation shapes what strategies are possible. Strategy reshapes what the organisation must become. The board must interrogate all three feedback loops — not just approve the plan.
The Innovation Trap

Strategy that succeeds creates an Organisation optimised for that success — which makes it resistant to the next Strategy the environment requires. Yesterday's winning O becomes today's strategic prison. The board's job is to see this feedback loop before management does.

Four EOS dynamics every board must track:

1

E–O Lag: Most performance failures are not strategy failures — they are Organisation failures to adapt to a changed Environment. Ask: "Is our organisation still suited to the environment we actually face today — not the one we planned for three years ago?"

2

S–E Feedback: In the Complex model, the firm's strategy actively shapes the environment — through standard-setting, ecosystem creation, regulatory influence, and competitor reactions. Boards must track not just how E affects S, but how S is changing E.

3

O–S Constraint: The organisation you have limits the strategies available to you. Culture, capabilities, and embedded mental models filter which strategic options management even brings to the board. The board must see the options management does not present — because the O has made them invisible.

4

Misalignment as Root Cause: When performance falls short, the instinct is to change S. Often the real problem is an O that cannot execute any S, or an E that has shifted without the board realising. Diagnosis before prescription: always start with the EOS fit assessment.

Shape vs. Adapt: The Commitment That Determines Everything

The Adapt/Shape dichotomy is the course's master insight — but it is frequently misunderstood as a preference rather than a commitment. The board's role is to force the choice and hold the organisation to it.

The Most Common Strategic Failure

Most firms have Shape rhetoric and Adapt organisation. They declare bold visions of industry transformation while running tight annual budgets, short-term incentives, and zero-tolerance-for-failure cultures. The mismatch is not hypocrisy — it is a governance failure. The board approved both without seeing the contradiction.

What Shape Requires
Patient capital with tolerance for loss. Long time horizons (5–15 years). Cognitive diversity on the board and in management. Organisational space for exploration. A serendipity-enabling culture. Principles-based governance. A board willing to absorb Absolute Uncertainty without demanding predictive plans.
What Adapt Requires
Precision execution against known benchmarks. Tight KPI frameworks. Short-cycle feedback loops. Operational excellence culture. A board skilled at variance analysis and holding management to plans. Sequential EOS discipline and comparative benchmarking.
Why the Middle Is Fatal
Attempting both simultaneously destroys both. Shape investments are killed by Adapt's quarterly discipline. Adapt's efficiency is undermined by Shape's experimental tolerance. The capabilities, cultures, incentives, and governance styles required are genuinely contradictory. Clarity of choice is not optional.
The Phase Transition Risk
Industries move through phases: Shaping at birth (disruptive innovation), then Adapting at maturity (operational excellence). Firms that founded on Shape often fail the governance transition to maturity. Adapt-optimised firms fail when disruption demands a Shape response. AI is forcing many mature industries back into a Shape phase right now.

Five board responsibilities in the Adapt/Shape choice:

1

Make it explicit: Put "Adapt or Shape — and why now?" on the board agenda at least annually. Most firms have never had this conversation at board level. The absence of the conversation is itself a governance failure.

2

Test organisational alignment: After the choice is made, audit whether the planning process, incentive structure, talent model, and culture actually support it. The gap between declared choice and operational reality is the most common source of strategic underperformance.

3

Calibrate board composition: A Shape board needs members with cognitive diversity, tolerance for ambiguity, and experience with Absolute Uncertainty. An Adapt board needs deep operational expertise and financial rigour. Most boards are not intentionally composed for their chosen approach.

4

Manage the transition actively: When an industry phase changes, the board must lead the governance transition — not react to it. This means changing incentives, planning horizons, and risk appetite before the crisis, not during it.

5

Protect path integrity: Once a choice is made, it reinforces itself through culture, capability investment, and talent selection. Switching is expensive and slow. Hold the line under competitive pressure — and only revisit the choice when the environment genuinely demands it, not when management is anxious.

Six Board Imperatives

🔍
1. Surface the Lens

Before evaluating strategy, evaluate the worldview behind it. What is management's mental model? Is it appropriate for the environment the firm actually faces?

⚖️
2. Demand the Diagnosis

Insist on a clear statement of the specific challenge being addressed. Good strategy begins with an honest diagnosis. Without it, everything else is cosmetic.

🎯
3. Enforce the Choice

Force the explicit choice between Adapt and Shape. Ensure every element of the organisation is aligned with the chosen approach. Resist the comfortable middle ground.

🛡️
4. Install Stop-Losses

Require pre-committed exit conditions on all major investments. Move exit decisions to moments of clear judgement, before sunk-cost bias takes hold.

🧭
5. Codify Principles

Ensure the firm has clear non-negotiables — what it will not do regardless of opportunity. Via negativa as strategic identity. This is what holds the organisation together under uncertainty.

⚡
6. Create Speed

In a world of technological acceleration and compressing strategy cycles, the board's greatest contribution is enabling rapid, confident action — not slowing it down with process.

These are the questions that distinguish a board operating in strategic mode from one operating in compliance mode:

The most diagnostic question in the course. Reveals whether management is paying attention to the edges of their model, where Absolute Uncertainty manifests.
◆

Forces a diagnosis before a plan. If management cannot answer in two sentences, there is no strategy — there is a wish list.
◆

The vpc lens. Strategies that only move the price point without growing the (v−c) pie are extractive and ultimately fragile.
◆

Forces rigour on corporate strategy transactions. Most claimed synergies are additive cost savings. Genuine value creation requires non-linear combination.
◆

The most common governance failure. When the environment is genuinely uncertain, probability-weighted scenarios are not just useless — they are dangerous because they create false confidence.
◆

The stop-loss discipline. Pre-commits exit before sunk-cost bias takes hold. The most consistently violated principle in corporate strategy.
◆

The ultimate question. The answer determines everything: planning style, organisation design, talent model, culture, and the board's own role.

A Vision for the Strategic Board

The boards that will create the most value over the next decade are not those with the best compliance frameworks. They are those that have made the deliberate choice between Adapt and Shape — and built everything else around that choice.

They understand that in a world of Absolute Uncertainty, the board's job is not to predict the future but to make the firm robust enough to act confidently within any future. This requires installing principles rather than plans, stop-losses rather than scenarios, and a culture that treats surprise as signal rather than noise.

The vpc framework keeps the board anchored to what matters: are we creating value for customers and shareholders simultaneously, or trading one off against the other? The EOS model reminds them that environment, organisation, and strategy are mutually constitutive — you cannot optimise one without affecting the others.

Above all, the strategic board makes the explicit choice: Adapt or Shape? And having made it, it holds the line — because the greatest destruction of strategic value comes not from choosing wrong, but from choosing nothing and drifting between both.

Synthesis II

Being a Board Member

The frameworks are only as good as the people applying them. This chapter translates the course's strategic theory into the practical realities of board membership — what the role actually demands, where it structurally fails, and what separates board members who add value from those who merely attend.

What the Role Actually Is

Board membership is the only senior leadership role defined by what you do not do. You do not manage. You do not execute. You do not run the operations. You govern — which means you create the conditions in which management can make good decisions, hold them accountable when they don't, and replace them when necessary.

This sounds straightforward. In practice it is one of the hardest roles in business, because it requires authority without operational control, accountability without information symmetry, and decisive judgement exercised six to ten times per year on the basis of curated board packs.

You Are a Fiduciary
Your legal duty is to act in the best interest of the company — which is not the same as acting in the interest of the CEO, the largest shareholder, or your own reputation. When these diverge, fiduciary duty must prevail. This is easier to state than to practice under boardroom social pressure.
You Are Collectively Responsible
Individual dissent must be voiced in the room, not outside it. A board decision is the board's decision — unanimous or not. If you cannot live with the outcome, you must either change it through persuasion or resign. Silence in the room followed by public distancing is a breach of the role.
You Are Part-Time with Full-Time Consequences
Most non-executive board members spend 30–60 days per year on a single board mandate. The consequences of what gets decided in those days can persist for a decade. The asymmetry between input and impact is extreme — and demands proportional preparation.
You Are Not an Advisor
An advisor gives counsel. A board member is accountable. The distinction matters: advisors can recommend without deciding; board members decide — and bear legal, reputational, and fiduciary consequences for those decisions. Treat every agenda item accordingly.

The Structural Information Problem

The most underestimated challenge in board work is not strategic complexity — it is information asymmetry. Management knows what is happening inside the firm. The board knows what management chooses to share. This gap is structural, permanent, and large.

The Filter Problem

Board packs are not reality — they are management's interpretation of reality, organised to support the narrative management wants the board to accept. Effective board members read between the lines: what is absent from the pack is often more revealing than what is present.

1

Design around the asymmetry: Request access to the CFO, CRO, and General Counsel outside of board meetings. Insist on site visits to operational locations. Commission independent reviews on critical matters. The board pack alone is structurally insufficient.

2

Conduct executive sessions: Regular meetings of the non-executive board without the CEO present are essential for honest assessment. This is where the real evaluation of management performance and CEO relationship health happens.

3

Do your own environmental scanning: Don't outsource your view of the industry to the management team. A board member who knows the environment only through management-filtered lenses is captive.

4

Track what surprised management: The "What surprised you since our last meeting?" question is powerful precisely because surprises reveal where Absolute Uncertainty is manifesting. A management team that is never surprised is not paying attention to the right signals.

The Dual Mandate: Support and Challenge

Every board member simultaneously holds two obligations in permanent tension.

Obligation 1
Support Management
  • Create conditions for management to succeed
  • Give clear strategic direction and boundaries
  • Back the CEO in difficult decisions
  • Provide expertise, networks, counsel
  • Protect management from short-term market pressure
  • Make timely decisions — don't create paralysis
⇄
Obligation 2
Challenge Management
  • Question the diagnosis behind the strategy
  • Surface what management may not be seeing
  • Hold management accountable to commitments
  • Represent shareholder and stakeholder interests
  • Act when performance is chronically inadequate
  • Be willing to replace the CEO when necessary
Where Boards Fail on the Dual Mandate

Too much support creates a rubber stamp — group think dominates, risks accumulate unseen. Too much challenge creates an adversarial dynamic — management becomes defensive and stops sharing bad news early. The effective board member reads which obligation is currently underprovided.

The CEO Relationship: The Most Consequential Dynamic

1

CEO selection is the board's most important decision. The board should maintain an active view of the internal talent pipeline and external alternatives at all times — not only during succession crises.

2

Performance evaluation must be structured, honest, and private. An annual CEO evaluation with clear criteria, candid delivery, and documented outcomes is a minimum standard.

3

Protect the CEO from short-termism — but not from accountability. Giving the CEO political cover to make long-term decisions is distinct from protecting the CEO from consequences of poor performance.

4

Know when the relationship has become too close. The most dangerous governance dynamic is a CEO who has captured the board through long tenure, personal relationships, or information control.

Synthesis III

New Board Member Integration

You have been appointed. Unlike an executive role, there is no performance review at 90 days, no ramp-up plan, no onboarding programme. The integration happens through the progressive application of the right lenses — not through a checklist. This chapter maps the course's frameworks onto the questions a new board member must answer to genuinely understand the firm and their place in governing it.

Start With Your Own Lens

The first lesson of the course is that outcomes reflect the cognitive lenses of the people at the top. The Upper Echelons insight applies to you, as a new board member, before it applies to anyone else. Before you can usefully interrogate management's mental model, you must know your own.

What Is Your Worldview?
Are you predominantly Reductionist — do you instinctively decompose problems into parts, seek measurable data, and trust predictive models? Or Systemic — do you look for interdependencies, emergence, and non-linear causality? Neither is superior. But not knowing which you are is dangerous, because your worldview will filter everything you see on this board without your awareness.
What Gap Were You Appointed to Fill?
You were appointed for a reason. Be explicit with yourself about what cognitive or experiential gap on this board you are intended to fill. That gap defines where your contribution will be most valuable — and where the board's collective blind spot currently sits. This is not humility; it is strategic self-positioning.
What Does Your Lens Cause You to Miss?
Every strength in a cognitive lens creates a corresponding blind spot. A career CFO will have sharp instincts on financial risk and weak instincts on culture and emergence. A founding entrepreneur will see opportunity and underweight systemic risk. Know your blind spots explicitly — or they will govern you from below the surface.

Read the Board's Collective Mental Model

Before your first substantive contribution, observe the board as a system. The Upper Echelons insight applies to the board collectively, not just to management. The board itself has a dominant worldview — and it shapes which risks get identified, which strategic options get considered, and which questions never get asked.

1

Is the board predominantly Reductionist or Systemic? Listen for how the board frames performance shortfalls. "Which KPI missed?" is Reductionist. "Which feedback loop broke down?" is Systemic. The language reveals the model.

2

Does the board operate in Risk or Absolute Uncertainty mode? Watch how the board discusses the future. Does it reach for scenario models and probability estimates (Risk)? Or does it reason from principles and accept that outcomes cannot be predicted (Absolute Uncertainty)? Most boards default to Risk tools — even when the problems they are discussing are genuinely Uncertain.

3

Is the board running System 1 or System 2 thinking? Are decisions made on pattern recognition and speed — or do they slow down, challenge assumptions, and reason from first principles? A board that never pauses is almost certainly using System 1 for decisions that require System 2.

4

What does the board never question? Every group has sacred assumptions — things that are never put on the agenda, positions that are held without examination, conclusions that are reached too quickly. Identify them. They are the highest-risk items in the firm's governance, precisely because no one is looking at them.

Diagnose the Firm Through EOS

The EOS model is your primary diagnostic instrument as a new board member. Apply it independently — before management tells you what the strategy is. Form your own view of each element, then assess the alignment between them.

E — What Environment Is the Firm Actually In?
Not the environment described in the strategic plan — the one the firm is actually operating in today. What are the competitive dynamics? Where is technology in its disruption cycle for this sector? Which globalisation phase is the industry in? What is the genuine uncertainty regime — Risk or Absolute? Form this view from your own reading, not from the board pack.
O — What Is the Organisation Actually Capable Of?
Not the capability described in management presentations — the real capability the organisation has demonstrated. What has it consistently done well? What has it consistently failed to execute? Culture and capability are revealed by history, not by stated intentions. The O often constrains strategy options in ways management is reluctant to admit.
S — What Is the Strategy Actually Doing?
Not the strategy stated in the plan — the strategy revealed by where resources are actually flowing. Capital allocation, management time, and hiring decisions reveal the real strategy. Ask: is the stated strategy the one the firm is actually executing? The gap between the two is a governance finding, not a management communication problem.
The EOS Alignment Question

After forming your independent view of E, O, and S, ask the central question: are they in coherent alignment? Is the strategy suited to the environment? Is the organisation capable of executing the strategy? Does the environment reward what the organisation is good at? Misalignment — in any combination — is the root cause of most sustained underperformance. Identifying it clearly is your first governance contribution.

The firm has Shape rhetoric and Adapt organisation. Management describes a bold vision for industry transformation while running tight annual budgets and zero-tolerance-for-failure incentives. Apply the vpc Lens to Understand Value

Before you can govern a firm's strategy, you must understand whether the strategy is creating value or extracting it. The vpc framework answers this question with precision.

1

Is the firm raising willingness to pay (v)? Is the competitive position strengthening — are customers willing to pay more for this product or service relative to alternatives? Expanding gross margins suggest yes. Compressing margins suggest the firm is becoming a commodity.

2

Is the firm lowering cost (c) structurally? Are cost advantages durable (scale, learning curves, proprietary processes) or one-time (restructuring, headcount reduction)? One-time savings are not a competitive advantage — they are borrowed time.

3

Is the total (v−c) pie growing? Or is the firm merely redistributing an existing pie between customers and shareholders through price moves? A strategy that only moves price without growing total value created is extractive — and eventually self-defeating as competitive dynamics erode it.

4

Is the claimed synergy emergence or aggregation? When evaluating acquisitions or partnership proposals, apply the integration test: does the combination create capabilities that neither party had alone (emergence, 1+1=3), or does it merely add their existing capabilities together (aggregation, 1+1=2)? The board should not approve transactions that claim emergence and deliver only aggregation.

Determine the Uncertainty Regime

One of the most consequential things a new board member must understand quickly: is this board operating in the domain of Risk or Absolute Uncertainty — and is it using the right tools for the regime it is actually in?

If the Board Is in Risk Mode
Scenario analysis, probability estimates, and risk matrices are appropriate. The board can usefully approve risk frameworks and set risk appetite. Management can be held to probabilistic targets. Standard governance tools apply.
If the Board Is in Absolute Uncertainty Mode
Scenario analysis produces false confidence. The correct tools are Non-Predictive: Precautionary Principle (avoid irreversible exposures), Stop-Loss rules (pre-commit exit conditions), Via Negativa (define what the firm will not do), and Principia (act from principles, not from plans). Most boards apply Risk tools to Absolute Uncertainty — this is the most common governance failure.
The Diagnostic Question

"What has surprised management since our last meeting?" asked in your first board meeting tells you everything about the uncertainty regime. If management is regularly surprised, the firm is operating under Absolute Uncertainty and the board must ensure it has the right framework. If management is never surprised, either the environment is unusually stable — or the board is not receiving honest reporting.

Read the Strategy for Coherence

Rumelt's diagnosis of good vs. bad strategy is the board's quality filter. Apply it to whatever strategic plan you inherit on joining.

A Good Strategy Has Three Elements
Diagnosis: a clear, honest statement of the specific challenge being faced. Guiding Policy: an approach that addresses the diagnosis. Coherent Actions: a set of moves that are mutually reinforcing and implement the policy. If any of these three is absent, you do not have a strategy — you have a plan, a vision, or a wish list.
Bad Strategy Warning Signs
Impressive language with no content ("we will be the leading provider of…"). Goals stated as strategy ("we will achieve 15% revenue growth"). Everything is a priority (which means nothing is). No hard choices made. No statement of what the firm will not do. Sound familiar? This is the Via Negativa failure — and it is endemic.

The Questions to Ask in Your First Meetings

These questions — drawn directly from the course frameworks — are the most diagnostic a new board member can deploy. They are not interview questions or due diligence checklists. They are the questions that reveal whether the firm is being well governed, whether the strategy is coherent, and where the real risks are hiding.

◆
"What has surprised you since the last board meeting?"
The course's single most powerful diagnostic question. Ask it in your first meeting. The answer tells you the uncertainty regime, management's honesty, and whether the board is receiving genuine information.
◆
"What is the specific challenge our strategy is designed to address?"
Rumelt's diagnosis test. If management cannot answer this in two sentences, there is no strategy. As a new board member, you have the standing to ask this naively — use it.
◆
"Are we shaping our environment or adapting to it — and is our organisation built for that choice?"
The master question of the course. It will likely be the first time this question has been asked at board level. The reaction it provokes is itself diagnostic.
◆
"Is the value we are creating growing the total pie, or are we redistributing an existing one?"
The vpc lens applied to the firm's trajectory. Distinguishes genuine value creation (v−c growing) from value redistribution (price moves and cost cuts that don't expand the pie).
◆
"What would have to be true for this strategy to fail — and have we pre-committed to a stop-loss if those conditions materialise?"
Non-Predictive Logic applied to major commitments. Forces the stop-loss conversation before sunk-cost bias makes it politically impossible.
◆
"What does this firm refuse to do — regardless of opportunity?"
Via Negativa. Strategic identity is defined as much by what a firm will not do as by what it pursues. If management cannot answer this, the firm has no strategic identity — only ambitions.
